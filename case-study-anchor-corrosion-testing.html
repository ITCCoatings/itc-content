ITC 213 Anchor Corrosion Testing: Alkali Resistance Results on 7 Steel Grades

Every Steel Grade Improved at Least One Quality Level -- Uncoated Inconel 601 Destroyed, ITC-Coated Retained Structure

Increased use of waste-derived alternative fuels in cement kilns has created a severe corrosion problem: high levels of alkali chlorides and sulphates attack stainless steel refractory anchors, causing premature failure and forcing the industry backward toward brick construction. ITC Coatings tested its ITC 213 ceramic coating on seven steel grades under accelerated corrosion conditions. The results: every single grade improved by at least one quality level, with some jumping from "Terrible" to "Acceptable" and others from "Excellent" to "Outstanding."

The Problem: Alkali Corrosion Is Destroying Refractory Anchors

The cement industry's shift toward waste-derived alternative fuels (tires, solvents, municipal waste) has introduced higher concentrations of alkali chlorides and sulphates into kiln environments. These compounds aggressively attack the stainless steel anchors that hold monolithic refractory linings in place.

The challenge is compounded by a fundamental contradiction: the steel grades with the best corrosion resistance often have service temperature limits too low for the kiln environment. Plant operators are forced to choose between corrosion resistance and temperature capability -- and neither option works well alone.

As a result, there is an increasing movement back toward traditional brick construction, abandoning the high-performance monolithic refractories that modern kiln designs require. Current mitigation strategies focus on modifying lining construction to limit gas tracking and minimizing insulation to avoid dew point conditions at the anchor interface.

The Test: ITC 213 vs. Alkali Corrosion

Method

Castable pots made of silicon carbide (SiC) were core drilled and filled with 40 grams of a 60:40 molar mix of Na₂SO₄ (sodium sulphate) and NaCl (sodium chloride) -- representative of the alkali environment inside alternative-fuel cement kilns.

Fourteen anchor samples were cut -- two of each steel grade. Half were coated with ITC 213; the other half remained uncoated as controls. Coating preparation was simple:

  1. Anchors rubbed with emery paper
  2. Rinsed in bleach
  3. ITC 213 applied by sponge (light coat)
  4. Dried for 24 hours

All samples were placed in individual SiC pots, covered with SiC lids, and heated in a furnace at 900°C (1,652°F) for 5 hours. After cooling, samples were visually inspected.

The Most Dramatic Result: Inconel 601

Inconel 601 is one of the most expensive and supposedly corrosion-resistant alloys available. In this test, the uncoated Inconel 601 was completely destroyed -- rated "Terrible" with its structure entirely corroded. The ITC-coated Inconel 601 still retained its original structure -- rated "Acceptable."

This single result demonstrates that even the most premium alloys cannot withstand alkali corrosion without protection, while a relatively simple ceramic coating can preserve the anchor's integrity.

The Cost Implication: Use Cheaper Steel

Perhaps the most commercially significant finding: ITC 213 enables the use of lower-cost steel grades in corrosive environments. When coated AISI 316 (a common, affordable grade) outperforms uncoated Inconel 601, the material cost savings are enormous. Plants no longer need to specify premium alloys for corrosive zones -- they can use standard grades with ITC 213 protection and achieve superior results.

Industries Affected

Alkali corrosion of refractory anchors is not limited to cement kilns. The same chemistry affects:

  • Waste-to-energy (WTE) facilities -- chlorinated waste streams create aggressive alkali environments
  • Biomass power plants -- agricultural waste contains high alkali content
  • Coal-fired power generation -- sulfur and chlorine compounds in coal
  • Petrochemical operations -- sulfur-containing process gases

Any industry using monolithic refractory linings with stainless steel anchors in environments containing alkali chlorides or sulphates can benefit from ITC 213 anchor protection.
